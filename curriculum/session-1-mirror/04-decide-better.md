## The Problem (0:15 - 1:30)

You make decisions every day that shape your life.

"I'm going to take that contract." "I'm going to pass on that opportunity." "I'm going to hire someone." "I'm going to move cities."

These are not small choices. They redirect your entire trajectory.

And yet: almost nobody writes them down.

Not the decision itself. I mean the full anatomy of it. The context you were in. The options you considered. The risks you weighed. The reason you chose what you chose.

Why does this matter?

Because in six months, you won't remember why you decided what you decided. You'll only remember the outcome. And if the outcome is bad, you'll blame yourself for a choice that may have been perfectly rational at the time.

Worse: you'll make the SAME type of decision again without the benefit of learning from the last one.

I learned this the hard way. I once turned down a partnership opportunity that, two years later, turned out to be exactly the right move. But I couldn't remember WHY I'd said no. I'd lost the reasoning. All I had was a vague memory of "it didn't feel right."

"It didn't feel right" is not a decision. It's a coin flip with a story attached.

If I'd written it down, I would have had a reusable decision lens: a pattern I could trust and refine over time.

---

## The Principle (1:30 - 2:45)

Every significant decision deserves a record. Not a diary entry. A structured record.

Here's what goes in it:

**Context**: What was happening when you made this decision? What pressures, deadlines, and emotions were active? Context decays fastest, so write it while it's fresh.

**Options**: What alternatives did you actually consider? Most people only remember the option they chose. The rejected options are where the learning lives.

**Risks**: What could go wrong? For each option, what's the worst case? And how reversible is the damage? A decision that's easily undone deserves less agonizing than one that isn't.

**Decision**: What did you choose, and in one sentence, why?

**Review Date**: When will you revisit this decision to see if it's still working? This is the most neglected part. Decisions don't expire, but the world they were made in does.

Five fields. Context, Options, Risks, Decision, Review Date.

This takes ten minutes. And it gives you something invaluable: a library of your own judgment.

Over time, you start to see your decision patterns. You notice: "I'm good at career decisions but terrible at financial ones." "I decide well under pressure but poorly when I have too much time." "I ignore relationship risks." Your library becomes a mirror that reflects back how your mind actually works.
