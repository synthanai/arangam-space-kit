# The 5 Stages of Systemic Competence

**A diagnostic framework for understanding where your organization really is.**

---

## Origin

This model extends the classic Burch/Gordon "4 Stages of Competence" (1970s) with a critical 5th stage that captures the greatest threat to successful organizations.

---

## The 5 Stages

### Stage 1: Unconscious Incompetence (Hero Culture)

**Symptom**: "We don't have a problem."

The organization doesn't know it lacks a system. Crises are handled by heroic individuals who work nights and weekends to save the day. When the hero burns out or leaves, the company scrambles.

**How to recognize it**:
- One person holds all the critical knowledge
- There are no documented processes
- "We've always done it this way"
- New employees take months to become productive because nothing is written down

**The exit**: See the problem. Acknowledge the hero dependency.

---

### Stage 2: Conscious Incompetence (The Tool Trap)

**Symptom**: "We bought Jira/Notion/Monday but nothing changed."

The organization now knows it has a problem, but thinks buying software will solve it. Tools are adopted enthusiastically and abandoned within 6 months because the underlying workflow was never designed.

**How to recognize it**:
- Multiple half-adopted tools ("we never fully set it up")
- People reverting to email and spreadsheets
- "The tool doesn't fit our workflow" (the workflow was never defined)

**The exit**: Recognize that tools implement systems; they don't replace them. Design the workflow first.

---

### Stage 3: Conscious Competence (The Bureaucracy Trap)

**Symptom**: "Our processes work, but they're exhausting."

The organization has built systems, but they require constant administrative energy. Every action needs a meeting, an approval, a form. The system works, but it's rigid and draining. Your best people start leaving because "there's too much process."

**How to recognize it**:
- Meetings about meetings
- SOPs that nobody reads but everyone must follow
- "I spend more time documenting than doing"
- Innovation is slow because every change requires committee approval

**The exit**: Move from rigid systems to organic ones. Build trust into the architecture so oversight becomes implicit, not explicit.

---

### Stage 4: Unconscious Competence (Invisible Architecture)

**Symptom**: "Things just work."

The organization has built systems that operate on trust, implicit coordination, and shared mental models. The architecture is invisible because it's embedded in culture. New employees absorb the way of working by osmosis. Coordination happens without meetings.

**How to recognize it**:
- People make good decisions without being told to
- Onboarding is fast because the system teaches itself
- Trust is high, bureaucracy is low
- The organization can adapt to change quickly

**The danger**: This is not the final stage. Success breeds complacency.

---

### Stage 5: Competent Incompetence (The Innovator's Trap)

**Symptom**: "We're the best. There's nothing left to learn."

This is the most *dangerous* stage. The organization has become so good at what it does that it has stopped questioning itself. Past success has created an identity: "We are the market leader." This identity resists any signal that the world has changed.

**How to recognize it**:
- "That's not how we do things here"
- New hires with fresh ideas are dismissed as naive
- The market is shifting but leadership dismisses the signals
- Internal metrics look great, but customers are slowly leaving

**Historical examples**: Kodak, Blockbuster, Nokia, BlackBerry.

**The exit**: Build a reflection loop that continuously audits your own success assumptions. This is what Pillar 7 (Learn Better) exists to provide.

---

## Using This Framework

1. **Diagnose**: Where is your team right now? Be honest. Use the [System Health Check](../kit/operations/system-health-check.md).
2. **Target**: What stage are you trying to reach? (Stage 4 is the goal; Stage 5 is the trap.)
3. **Plan**: What specific changes will move you from your current stage to the next?
4. **Monitor**: Set a 90-day review to reassess your stage.
